# From Curiosity to Plans

After the first spark, I did not immediately start building LuckyPlans.

At that point, the idea was still simple:

**“Can I copy a profitable on-chain trader?”**

Interesting question.

Also dangerous.

Because simple trading ideas usually look clean right before they become expensive.

So I started researching.

And very quickly, I learned the obvious thing every founder learns slightly later than his ego prefers:

Other people had already thought about copy trading.

Binance had copy trading. Copin.io was tracking on-chain traders. Other platforms already had leaderboards, trader profiles, win rates, realized PnL, and follower-style systems.

For a moment, that was disappointing.

You think you found a new island, then Google shows you hotels, airports, and three competitors with better logos.

But after that first disappointment, I realized something important.

The market already existed.

People understood the idea. Users already wanted to follow traders. Copy trading was not just a strange thought I had after staring at a leaderboard for too long.

So the question changed.

It was no longer:

**“Is this idea new?”**

It became:

**“What is still missing?”**

That was a much better question.

## The Problem Before the Copy Button

Most copy trading products are easy to understand.

Find a trader. Check the stats. Click follow. Copy the trades.

That simplicity is powerful.

But the more I looked at the space, the more I became interested in what happens **before** the follow button.

How do you discover enough traders?
How do you compare them beyond one attractive number?
How do you know if a trader is still active, not just historically impressive?
How do you test an idea before putting real money behind it?

That was where LuckyPlans started to feel different.

The hard part was not only copying.

The hard part was choosing what deserved to be copied.

A copy button is simple.

A decision system is not.

And in trading, the difference between those two can become expensive very quickly.

## One Trader Was Too Fragile

The original idea was emotionally simple:

**“Copy a good trader.”**

But one trader is still one point of failure.

One trader can look amazing on a leaderboard. One trader can have beautiful numbers. One trader can make you open a spreadsheet at midnight and briefly believe you have discovered the next financial operating system.

But if everything depends on that one trader, then you are trusting one address to keep the same discipline, same edge, same timing, and same behavior.

That felt too fragile.

At that time, I did not yet have a mature risk framework. I was not thinking deeply about slippage, execution delay, market regime changes, or hidden tail risk.

Those lessons were still waiting in the future.

With invoices.

But even then, the better question became:

**“What if LuckyPlans should not copy one trader, but organize multiple traders?”**

That question changed the product.

## The First Real Model: Plans

The first real solution was not a better copy button.

It was a plan.

Instead of one bot following one trader, LuckyPlans could group multiple bots under one structure. Each bot could follow a different trader or represent a different copy behavior.

The user would not just follow someone.

The user would build a copy trading plan.

That distinction mattered.

A copy button says:

**“I trust this trader.”**

A plan says:

**“This is how I want to structure my exposure.”**

One trader might be the core. Another might be experimental. Another might add a different trading style. Another might deserve only a small allocation because the numbers are interesting, but not interesting enough to let it drive the car.

Suddenly, LuckyPlans was not only about copying.

It was about composition.

## Ratio-Based Sizing

Once multiple traders entered the model, the next question was obvious:

**How much weight should each trader get?**

Equal weighting felt too lazy.

Not every trader should have the same influence. Some might deserve more confidence. Some might be useful only as small exposure. Some might be worth watching, but not worth giving the steering wheel.

So I started thinking in ratios.

A simple plan could look like this:

**Trader A: 50%**
**Trader B: 30%**
**Trader C: 20%**

Nothing magical.

But it changed the product from passive following into active construction.

The user was no longer saying:

**“I follow this trader.”**

The user was saying:

**“This is my plan.”**

That small language change mattered.

A plan can be adjusted. Tested. Compared. Improved. Simulated.

A simple follow action either works or hurts you.

A plan gives you something to study.

That was when LuckyPlans started to feel like a product instead of a trading shortcut.

## Why the Name Made Sense

This was also when the name **LuckyPlans** started to feel right.

The “lucky” part came from the original energy of the idea — seeing a great trader and thinking, “Maybe I can follow that luck.”

But the “plans” part made it serious.

The product was not supposed to be **LuckyTraderCopyButton.com**.

Fortunately, I did not register that domain.

Rare early risk control.

The real idea was structured plans from public trader behavior:

Multiple traders.
Different ratios.
Organized bots.
Eventually, simulation and backtesting.

The name had a playful surface, but the model underneath was becoming disciplined.

That balance mattered.

Copy trading may start with curiosity.

But once money enters the room, the product needs structure.

## The First Product Shape

By the end of this phase, I still had more questions than answers.

But the direction was clear.

LuckyPlans was not going to be just another “follow this trader” product.

It needed trader discovery, multiple-trader planning, ratio-based sizing, public data analysis, simulation before trust, and backtesting before real execution.

That was the first real product shape.

Not finished.

Not validated.

Not ready for real capital.

But clear enough to build.

The first trader created the spark.

The research showed me the market.

But the plan model gave LuckyPlans its direction.

That was the moment the idea stopped being about copying one person and started becoming a system for organizing trader behavior.

And once I saw that structure, the next step was no longer more thinking.

It was time to build.
