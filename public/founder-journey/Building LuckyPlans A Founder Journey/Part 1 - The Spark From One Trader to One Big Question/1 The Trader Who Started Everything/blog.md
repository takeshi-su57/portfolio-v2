# The Trader Who Started Everything

LuckyPlans did not start with a pitch deck.

It did not start with a market thesis.

And it definitely did not start with a clean founder story.

It started with one leaderboard that made me stop scrolling.

Around May 2024, I was working as a frontend engineer on a perp DEX project for the Botanix chain. The product was based on a Gains Network-style perp engine, and my work was mostly close to the interface layer.

I was building trading pages, connecting smart contract data, wiring transactions with wagmi and viem, and trying to make protocol complexity feel usable for normal users.

It was technical work, but familiar technical work.

Then one day, I opened the Gains Network leaderboard.

And I saw one trader.

More than **$500K** in realized profit.

Very high win rate.

And a public trading history I could inspect directly.

That last part mattered.

This was not a screenshot on Twitter.

Not a marketing thread.

Not a cropped PnL image with all the inconvenient context removed.

It was on-chain.

The address was visible.
The trades were visible.
The history was visible.
The profit was visible.

A lot of people in crypto say they make money.

But this time, I could actually check the history myself.

So my first reaction was simple:

**“Is this real?”**

Then almost immediately, the more dangerous question came:

**“If it is real… can I copy him?”**

That was the first LuckyPlans moment.

Not a product yet.

Not a company.

Not even a real plan.

Just one uncomfortable question sitting inside public data.

## The Shift

The idea looked obvious at first.

A trader opens a position.

The protocol emits an event.

The event is public.

A system can watch it.

And if the system can watch it, maybe the system can react.

That logic was simple enough to be exciting.

Also simple enough to be suspicious.

At the time, I did not understand all the details that would make the idea difficult later: execution delay, sizing mismatch, RPC reliability, liquidity, slippage, fees, trader behavior changes, and the painful difference between seeing a good trade and copying it well.

All of that came later.

The first insight was simpler.

It was about transparency.

In traditional trading, you rarely get this kind of visibility. You may see performance claims, signals, reports, or commentary after everything already happened. But the actual behavior is usually hidden.

On-chain trading changes that.

Trader behavior leaves a trail.

Not a perfect trail.

Not an easy trail.

But a visible one.

And that changed how I saw the leaderboard.

Before that moment, a leaderboard was just a ranking page.

After that moment, it looked like raw material.

A trader address was not just a wallet.

It was a behavioral record.

An event log was not just backend noise.

It was a possible signal.

A public trading history was not just a stat page.

It was something that could be organized, tested, and maybe turned into a product.

The first question was:

**“Can I copy this trader?”**

But the deeper question was already forming:

**“Can public trading behavior become usable infrastructure?”**

That was the real beginning.

## The Second Look

I did not start building immediately.

I continued my normal work. There were still features to ship, contracts to connect, and enough frontend details to remind me that software does not politely pause just because you had an idea.

But the question kept coming back.

A few months later, after August 2024, I checked the same trader again.

Part of me expected the story to be over.

Maybe the first result was just a good period.

Maybe the market had helped him.

Maybe the leaderboard had captured one temporary anomaly.

But he was still there.

Still active.

Still profitable.

Still growing.

His total profit was getting close to **$1M USDC**.

That changed the weight of the idea.

The first time, it was curiosity.

The second time, it became harder to ignore.

And then the uncomfortable question appeared:

**“What would have happened if I had copied him earlier?”**

That is not always a healthy question.

But it is powerful.

It turns an idea from abstract into personal.

Suddenly, the gap between observing and acting becomes very visible.

I still did not know if the idea would work.

I did not know if it could become a product.

I did not know if copying public trader behavior would survive real execution.

But I knew the question was strong enough to investigate.

## Why It Mattered

Looking back, the trader himself was not the real point.

He was the trigger.

What mattered was the change in perspective.

I stopped seeing on-chain trading data as something that only supported dashboards.

I started seeing it as a product layer by itself.

Public trading history could support discovery.

Discovery could support comparison.

Comparison could support plans.

Plans could support simulation.

And simulation could eventually support safer execution.

That full chain was not clear yet.

But the direction was there.

LuckyPlans was not born from certainty.

It was born from one question that was too interesting to ignore, and too practical to leave in a notes app forever.

One trader made the data feel alive.

The rest of the journey started from that.
