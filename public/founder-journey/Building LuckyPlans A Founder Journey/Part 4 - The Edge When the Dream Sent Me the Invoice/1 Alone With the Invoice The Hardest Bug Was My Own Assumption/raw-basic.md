Blog 1 — Alone With the Invoice: The Hardest Bug Was My Own Assumption
Time period

October 2025 – November 2025

Main content

This blog should start after the V2 wall.

LuckyPlans had technically become stronger, but the result was painful. After the copy trading plan failed, your brother Hiroshi had to step back because he was busy with his own business.

That made the failure feel heavier.

Before, the journey was shared.
Now, you were alone with the result.

This blog should focus on the emotional and mental reset after failure.

You were depressed. You questioned the plan. You questioned whether LuckyPlans was a real startup direction or just an expensive experiment. But instead of fully walking away, you started researching why this failed.

That research led to a painful realization:

Maybe the problem was not only execution.
Maybe the original copy trading assumption itself was weak.

You found similar stories from copy traders and articles. The pattern was uncomfortable:

A public trader looks great.
The PnL graph looks stable.
Followers enter after the beautiful part is already visible.
Then the trader suddenly takes a big loss.
Or, if the trader is truly great, they may not stay publicly copyable forever.

The key idea:

A great-looking leader is not automatically a great copy target.

This blog should show that you were not just debugging code anymore. You were debugging the belief that started LuckyPlans.

Core lesson

The hardest bug was not in the indexer, bot, or smart contract integration.

It was the assumption that finding a great-looking leader was enough.

Suggested ending

“For the first time, I was not debugging a contract, an indexer, or a bot. I was debugging the reason I believed this should work. The hardest bug was not in the codebase. It was in my assumption.”
